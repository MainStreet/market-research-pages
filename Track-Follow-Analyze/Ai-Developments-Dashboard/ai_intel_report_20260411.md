### 2. Anthropic Loses Appeals Court Bid on Pentagon Blacklisting
**Policy** | Regulatory Risk | AI Governance

**Summary:** A federal appeals court denied Anthropic's request to temporarily block the Department of Defense's "supply chain risk" designation. Anthropic became the first American company to receive this classification -- historically reserved for foreign adversaries like Huawei -- after refusing to grant the Pentagon unfettered access to its AI models for autonomous weapons and mass surveillance applications. Oral arguments are scheduled for May 19.

**Why It Matters:** This creates an unprecedented tension in AI governance: a leading American AI company is being treated as a national security risk by its own government for exercising safety principles. The precedent could force every frontier lab to choose between safety commitments and federal contract eligibility. If upheld, it effectively creates a government veto over AI safety decisions, fundamentally altering the relationship between frontier labs and the state.

**Use Cases:**
- Legal precedent for how governments can compel AI companies to provide model access
- Framework for other labs evaluating whether to accept military contracts with unrestricted access
- Test case for the limits of voluntary AI safety commitments under government pressure

**Investment Implications:** Creates regulatory uncertainty for the entire frontier AI sector. Anthropic's $30B revenue run rate demonstrates it can grow without DOD contracts, but exclusion from defense procurement could limit its addressable market. Other labs (OpenAI, Google DeepMind) may face similar demands. Defense-focused AI companies like Palantir and Anduril could benefit from reduced competition. Watch the May 19 oral arguments closely.

With 46 total bills passing in two weeks, a patchwork of state regulations is forming in the absence of federal preemption.

**Investment Implications:** Compliance costs will disproportionately affect smaller AI companies that lack dedicated legal and policy teams. Large frontier labs are better positioned to absorb regulatory overhead, potentially widening the moat. The NY RAISE Act's revenue threshold effectively creates a regulatory tier system that incumbents can navigate more easily than challengers.

## GLOSSARY (New Terms -- See Prior Reports for Full Glossary)

**TPU (Tensor Processing Unit)** -- Google's custom AI chip designed specifically for machine learning workloads. Unlike general-purpose GPUs, TPUs are optimized for the matrix operations that power neural networks. Anthropic's 3.5GW deal gives it massive TPU capacity through Google and Broadcom.

**Supply Chain Risk Designation** -- A DOD classification historically reserved for foreign adversaries (like Huawei) that bars a company from federal defense contracts. Anthropic became the first American company to receive this designation in March 2026 after refusing to grant the Pentagon unfettered access to its AI models for autonomous weapons and mass surveillance applications.

**Video-Action Model (VAM)** -- A robotics AI architecture that uses pretrained video generation models as a backbone to understand physical dynamics, then maps that understanding to robot actions. Unlike VLAs that learn physics from scratch, VAMs leverage internet-scale video data for significantly better sample efficiency.

**Vera Rubin** -- NVIDIA's next-generation GPU platform succeeding Blackwell, named after the astronomer. Both the CoreWeave-Meta ($21B) and CoreWeave-Anthropic ($6.8B) deals specify Vera Rubin systems, indicating pre-committed demand for hardware that has not yet shipped at scale.

**RAISE Act (NY S 8828)** -- New York's Responsible AI Safety and Evaluation Act, signed March 27, 2026, effective January 1, 2027. Requires frontier model developers with $500M+ annual revenue to publish safety plans and report AI incidents within 72 hours. First U.S. state law to impose revenue-threshold-based obligations on frontier AI labs.
